🤝 For Supporters

Supporting Someone Experiencing Abuse

How to help a friend, sister, neighbour, daughter-in-law, or elderly parent — safely, without making things worse.

✅ The most important thing you can do is listen without judgement and let the person make their own decisions. Trying to force a solution — however well-intentioned — can put them at greater risk.
👂 What to Say (and Not Say)+

Helpful things to say

  • "I believe you."
  • "This is not your fault."
  • "I'm here for you — whenever you're ready."
  • "What do you need right now?"
  • "I'll support you no matter what you decide."

Things to avoid saying

  • "Why don't you just leave?" — Leaving is dangerous and complicated. It is not simple.
  • "But he seemed so nice / I can't believe it." — This invalidates their experience.
  • "Think of the children / your in-laws / your family's honour." — She is already thinking of these. Adding to the pressure makes leaving harder.
  • "Have you tried talking to him more?" — This implies it is her fault for not communicating better.
  • "I told you so / I always knew he was bad." — Focus on the present, not on being right.
⚠️ Do not pressure her to leave before she is ready. Statistically, a victim leaves an abusive relationship an average of 7 times before leaving permanently. Each attempt where the support network fails or pressures her makes the next attempt harder.
🛡️ Practical Ways to Help+
  • Be a safe phone contact: Agree on a code word. If she uses it in a message, you know to call police.
  • Keep her documents safe: Offer to store copies of her Aadhaar, marriage certificate, and other key documents at your home.
  • Offer your home as a refuge: Make clear she can come to you at any time, day or night, without explanation.
  • Help with financial independence: Offer to let her store emergency cash at your home, help her open a bank account using your address.
  • Do not confront the abuser: This almost always escalates danger for the victim, not for you.
  • Do not tell others what she has shared with you without her permission. This can reach the abuser through unexpected routes.
👴👵 Supporting an Elderly Parent+

Elderly parents being abused by their own adult children face unique barriers — shame, financial dependence, fear of being seen as "troublemakers," and not knowing their rights.

Signs to watch for

  • Unexplained injuries, malnourishment, or poor hygiene
  • Withdrawal from family, friends, or activities they previously enjoyed
  • Fear or anxiety when the adult child is nearby
  • Sudden changes to wills, property transfers, or bank accounts
  • The elderly person is not allowed to speak privately

How to help

  • Speak to them privately — create an opportunity for them to speak without the adult child present.
  • Remind them of their legal rights under the Senior Citizens Act — they can file a complaint and their property transfers can be revoked.
  • Help them call Elderline: 14567 — a government helpline specifically for senior citizens.
  • If you are a neighbour, teacher, or family friend and you see signs, you can report to the local Tribunal for Senior Citizens without the elderly person's involvement.
🎓 If You Are a Teacher, Counsellor, or College Staff+
  • Believe the student. Research consistently shows that false disclosures of abuse are extremely rare.
  • Do not promise confidentiality you cannot keep — in some cases, you may have reporting obligations. Be honest about this upfront.
  • Do not contact parents if the abuse is coming from within the family — this can put the student at immediate risk.
  • Connect the student with your institution's counsellor and the Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) if applicable.
  • Provide them with the Childline number (1098) and iCall number (9152987821).
  • Allow flexibility with deadlines and attendance during the crisis period.
✅ Your role is to be a safe adult who listens and connects — not to solve the situation or investigate it. That is the job of trained professionals at Childline and 181.
💜 Looking After Yourself as a Supporter+

Supporting someone through abuse is emotionally exhausting. Secondary trauma (also called compassion fatigue) is real and common.

  • You are not responsible for fixing the situation or for the choices she makes.
  • Set limits on what you can offer — it is okay to say "I can't answer calls after midnight, but I'll call you first thing in the morning."
  • Talk to someone yourself — a trusted friend, or a counsellor. iCall (9152987821) supports supporters too.
  • Remember: she may go back. She may not be ready yet. Your continued, judgement-free presence is more valuable than any ultimatum.
